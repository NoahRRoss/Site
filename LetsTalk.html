The Business Case

Why This Role Needs to Exist — and Why Now

The wealth management industry is undergoing its most significant technology transformation in decades. According to Deloitte's 2024 wealth management outlook, over 70% of high-net-worth clients now expect a fully integrated digital experience — yet fewer than 30% of regional banks report having the internal infrastructure to consistently deliver it.

Old National Bank's PWM division is well-positioned to lead. But capturing that position requires more than reacting to technology changes as they arrive — it requires dedicated strategic leadership embedded within the business, accountable for driving the initiatives that will define our competitive standing over the next 3–5 years.

This role does not currently exist at Old National Bank. This proposal makes the case for why it should — and outlines the mandate, structure, and accountability model that will make it successful.

Why This Investment Is Justified Now

Three Converging Pressures

Client Expectations Have Shifted

High-net-worth clients benchmark their experience against fintechs and national wealth managers. Gaps in digital capability are no longer overlooked — they are reasons to leave.

Advisor Productivity Is Constrained

Time spent on manual workarounds, fragmented data, and inconsistent processes is time not spent deepening client relationships. This is a revenue problem, not just an efficiency problem.

Regulatory Demands Are Increasing

Data governance, reporting accuracy, and audit-readiness requirements continue to expand. A reactive posture to compliance technology is a growing liability.

Each of these is addressable — but not without dedicated leadership to own the work.

The Gap This Role Fills

What Exists Today vs. What This Role Adds

Today, IT and process leadership within PWM operates reactively. What is missing is a dedicated, senior-level function focused entirely on forward-looking strategy. This is not a reallocation of existing responsibilities — it is a net new capability.

Strategic Mandate

Three Pillars, Sequenced for Impact

The work of this role is organized around three pillars. Pillar 1 is the Year 1 priority. Pillars 2 and 3 are initiated in parallel as the role matures and operational foundations are set.

Pillar 1  ·  Year 1 Priority

Digital Client Experience

Clients expect a seamless, modern digital experience. The current toolset is fragmented and reactive — creating friction for advisors and inconsistency for clients at critical relationship moments.

Owns: PWM-specific digital roadmap with committed milestones; peer benchmarking of client portal and communication capabilities; business requirements and change management for all client-facing technology upgrades.

Measurable Outcomes

Reduction in advisor time on manual workarounds (target: [X]% within 12 months) · Improvement in client digital engagement scores · Reduction in client-reported technology complaints

Pillar 2  ·  Year 1–2

Data, Reporting & Advisor Enablement

Leadership and advisors are making decisions on manually assembled, often delayed data. The raw information exists — the gap is infrastructure and ownership to surface it reliably.

Owns: PWM data and reporting strategy with clear deliverable timelines; rationalization of existing reporting tools; automation of high-frequency recurring reports.

Measurable Outcomes

Reduction in time-to-report for key dashboards (target: [X] days to real-time) · Elimination of [X] manual reporting processes · Quantified advisor hours recovered through automation

Pillar 3  ·  Year 2

Process Optimization & Scalable Operations

Current processes were built for a smaller, less complex business. Manual handoffs, inconsistent workflows, and unclear ownership create compounding operational risk as volume grows.

Owns: Structured process audit across onboarding, account maintenance, and compliance touchpoints; automation through RPA and workflow tools; a continuous improvement framework with clear ownership.

Measurable Outcomes

Reduction in new advisor onboarding time (target: [X] days) · Elimination of [X] manual handoff points · Quantified reduction in operational error rates

Building for the Future

A Dedicated Innovation Function

Alongside the Director-level strategic role, this proposal includes the creation of a supporting position dedicated to keeping Old National Bank ahead of the technology curve — researching and piloting emerging tools before they become industry-standard requirements.

This role will focus on AI tools, automation platforms, client experience innovations, and adjacent technologies. It will translate capability into practical PWM use cases through low-risk proof-of-concept pilots, and serve as an internal thought partner when the team evaluates new vendors or approaches.

Budget & ROI: Fully-loaded cost estimated at $[X] annually. Expected return: reduced vendor spend through better-informed procurement, technology adoption in months rather than years, and avoided cost of reactive implementations — which industry data shows run 2–3x the cost of planned ones. Formal ROI review at 12 months.

Operating Structure: A weekly cross-functional standup will connect this role to every line of business and IT — a brief, action-oriented forum to surface emerging needs, share what is being piloted, and align on priorities before they become urgent.

Reporting Structure: This position reports directly to the COO or Director of Operations — not to a single line of business or IT. A role focused on cross-functional innovation cannot carry organizational credibility if perceived as owned by one department. This structure provides the visibility, authority, and neutrality needed to operate across all business units without constraint — and signals enterprise-level commitment to this work.

Operational Continuity

Transition Plan

The most important risk to address is continuity. This role does not succeed if existing operational coverage degrades during the stand-up period.

01

Months 1–3  ·  Transition

Daily operational responsibilities documented, assigned, and handed off. Escalation path remains available. No new strategic initiatives begin until handoffs are confirmed stable.

02

Months 3–6  ·  Parallel Running

Pillar 1 work begins while operations team runs independently. Bi-weekly check-in to catch and resolve any coverage gaps quickly.

03

Month 6+  ·  Full Activation

Director role operates at full strategic capacity. Operational escalations handled through established team structure — engaged only for cross-functional or strategic implications.

Governance & Accountability

Structure That Keeps This Honest

Decision rights are clear: this role owns initiative execution and recommendations. Senior leadership retains final authority on prioritization, resourcing, and scope changes that cross business lines.

  • Initiative Roadmap — Living document of active and pipeline projects with owners, milestones, status, and risks. Updated monthly and shared with senior leadership.
  • Monthly Progress Updates — Concise written update on each pillar covering milestones, decisions needed, and emerging risks. Escalations flagged within 48 hours.
  • Quarterly Business Reviews — Standing agenda item with senior leadership to assess pillar alignment and course-correct before misalignment compounds.
  • Underperformance Protocol — If a pillar falls materially behind target, a root-cause review is completed within 30 days. No initiative runs more than one quarter behind without a formal continue / restructure / deprioritize decision.
  • Annual Role Review — Structured assessment of whether focus areas remain the right ones, conducted jointly with the COO and relevant business line leaders.
